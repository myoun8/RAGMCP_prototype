# Various Examples of the Types of Measurement Capabilities on BT7

**Objectives:** This document provides examples and brief explanations of the types of measurements that can be carried out on the BT-7 instrument.

## I. Powder Diffraction Using the PSD

The Position Sensitive Detector (PSD) can be used with a radial collimator and no energy analyzer to obtain powder diffraction patterns.

### Case Study: $\text{LaFeAsO}$
One of the first measurements of this type was performed on $\text{LaFeAsO}$, the parent material of iron-based high $T_C$ superconductors. 
* **Configuration:** Coarse resolution, high intensity to search for weak magnetic peaks.
* **Observations:** Strong structural peaks are present; at low temperatures, weak magnetic peaks appear at scattering angles of approximately 26, 34, and 59 degrees.

### Polarized Beam Techniques
Polarized beam techniques are used to unambiguously identify the origin of peaks (magnetic vs. structural).
* **Non-spin-flip (NSF) vs. Spin-flip (SF):** Nuclear coherent scattering (structural) never causes a spin-flip. Therefore, structural peaks appear only in the NSF geometry.
* **Magnetic Scattering:** Magnetic scattering can cause a spin-flip.
* **Practical Application:** Because polarizing the beam and analyzing polarization results in substantial intensity loss, this technique is typically used once to uniquely identify the nature of the scattering.

The intensity of a magnetic peak is directly proportional to the square of the ordered magnetic moment (the order parameter of the phase transition).

## II. Single Crystal Diffraction with the PSD

BT-7 can be used to map scattering planes for single crystals. 

### Case Study: $\text{Co}_3\text{TeO}_6$
Measurements of the $(H, K, 0)$ scattering plane for the multiferroic $\text{Co}_3\text{TeO}_6$ revealed:
* **Below 26 K:** Initial magnetic ordering with four incommensurate magnetic satellite peaks surrounding fundamental structural Bragg peaks.
* **At 19.5 K:** Commensurate magnetic peaks develop alongside incommensurate satellites.
* **At 18 K:** Changes in commensurate and incommensurate structures; ferroelectricity develops.
* **At 16 K:** Final changes in magnetic structures; ferroelectricity is lost.

## III. Polarized Beam Single Crystal Diffraction

### Case Study: $\text{Ni}_3\text{V}_2\text{O}_8$ (NVO)
NVO is an insulating multiferroic magnet. Polarized neutron scattering was used to determine the "handiness" (chirality) of its cycloidal magnetic structure.
* **Mechanism:** By cooling the sample in an applied electric field $E$ of $+300\text{ V}$ versus $-300\text{ V}$, the domains of the cycloidal magnetic structure can be controlled.
* **Observation:** This results in an opposite polarized intensity asymmetry in the spin-flip scattering conditions.

## IV. Inelastic Magnetic Scattering

Dynamics are investigated via inelastic scattering using a fixed final energy ($E_f = 14.7\text{ meV}$) while varying the incident energy ($E_i > E_f$). This allows the measurement of magnons, phonons, excitons, or crystal field levels.

### Polycrystalline Samples: $\text{Ba}_{0.56}\text{Sr}_{0.44}\text{MnO}_3$
* **Method:** PSD used at a single angular setting while varying temperature.
* **Results:** Antiferromagnetic ordering at $200\text{ K}$. Inelastic scattering (measured in horizontal energy focusing mode) revealed a spin gap of $4.6(5)\text{ meV}$ and a magnon density-of-states peak at $43\text{ meV}$.

### Single Crystal Samples: $\text{Fe}_{0.835}\text{Ga}_{0.165}$
Single crystals (typically gram-size) allow for the direct measurement of magnon dispersion relations rather than just the density-of-states.

### Polarized Inelastic Scattering
To resolve ambiguity between nuclear coherent scattering (phonons/structural) and magnetic scattering:
* **Nuclear Coherent Scattering:** Always non-spin-flip.
* **Magnetic Scattering:** Spin-flip intensity is maximal when the polarization $P$ is parallel to the scattering vector $Q$ ($P \parallel Q$).
* **Example:** In $\text{YBa}_2\text{Cu}_3\text{O}_{6.6}$, spin-flip scattering at an energy transfer of $34\text{ meV}$ demonstrates the magnetic origin of the resonant excitation.

## Lattice Dynamics

Phonon dispersion relations for single crystals are measured similarly to magnons.

### Case Study: $\text{PMN-30\%PT}$
Measurements along the $[0, 1, 0]$ direction revealed:
* Transverse Optical (TO) and Transverse Acoustic (TA) phonon branches.
* Intrinsic localized modes between the TA and TO branches, associated with polar nanoregions in relaxor ferroelectrics.

### Measurement Techniques
1. **Conventional Triple Axis:** Horizontally flat monochromator and analyzer with Söller collimators. Provides best signal-to-noise but collects data point-by-point.
2. **Double Focusing/PSD Mode:** Radial collimator after the sample, flat analyzer, and PSD. This allows the instrument to "cut through" scattering (e.g., lattice polaron scattering in $\text{La}_{0.7}\text{Ca}_{0.3}\text{MnO}_3$) for a single spectrometer setting, coupling $Q$ and energy transfer.
